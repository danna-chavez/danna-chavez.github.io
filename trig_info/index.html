Unit Circle

In Algebra 1 you get a taste of trigonometric functions. You are probably familiar with the mnemonic “SOHCAHTOA”. The sine, cosine, and tangent of an angle can be found corresponding to the sides of a right triangle. This concept is very limited in terms of searching for angles because it only works if the angles are acute since a triangle can’t have two ninety degree angles. The concept of a right triangle is still accurate but it’s a picture that’s been zoomed. If you zoom out, you’ll see the right triangle is actually inscribed in the first quadrant of a coordinate plane. Sine and Cosine are functions that turn an angle, theta, along the perimeter of a circle with the radius of one.

Cotangent

The reciprocal identity of cotangent is also useful to know. It is not just the reciprocal of tan but it can also be rewritten:

Cot(θ) = cos(θ)/sin(θ)

Because we have established that Tan(θ) = Sin(θ) / Cos(θ) and that Cot is the reciprocal of Tan, we can divided one by sin(θ)/cos(θ) and get cos(θ)/sin(θ)

Pythagorean Theorem

Although we accredit the greek mathematician Pythagoras for this theorem, the concept of “ the sum of the squares on the legs of a right triangle is equal to the square of the hypotenuse” was utilized before Pythagoras was born. There are written accounts on Egyptian papyrus that date back to 2000 BC that contain a pythagorean triple solution. It was a crucial component to Egyptian architecture. Not only the Egyptians, but the Sumerians used it for the infrastructure of Mesopotamia. This law of nature was discovered throughout many ancient civilizations at distinctive moments in history. This law is the backbone of trigonometry and can not only be applied to several situations but can also be proven in several ways.
